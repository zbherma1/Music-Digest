God In The Waiting


What makes a concert a concert? Is it the number of people there, all listening to your every
note? Or is it the level of engagement from the audience?

That is the question that Christian artist Seth Jernigan faced at his concert last Tuesday at 7 p.m.
as people slowly trickled into the A.T.O. Chapel.

By the time the concert started 15 minutes later, there were only 20 people in attendance.

But Jernigan didn't let the amount of people, or the lack thereof, deter him.

“This is gonna be super intimate, gonna be super interactive, and I would love for it to be that
way,” Jernigan said.

Jernigan started the concert with a short summary of who he was (a native of Pittsburgh, Penn.),
what he was doing there (touring to promote his new album, God in the Waiting), and how he
was feeling (super excited to share the album).

And then he immediately went off script, asking the audience if there was a song that they would
like to sing to start off the night, and an audience member suggested “The Stand” by Hillsong.

After kicking off the night in corporate worship, Jernigan began to play his original songs,
opening with “More of You.”

The night was filled with songs with a multitude of sources of inspiration, like “Psalm 27,”
which is based on Psalms 27 and the Presbyterian tradition of singing Psalms a capella.

Dean Whitcher, his pianist, would occasionally contribute vocally, whether to sing himself, like
he did in “Lord Please Stay,” or to add back-up vocals like in “Resurrection Life.”

As Jernigan explained the story behind each of his songs, he gave credit to everyone who had
helped him with in the production of his album, such as Whitcher, who created the piano and
bass tracks, and Emily Rodgers down in Nashville who recorded the cello used in “Make Me a
Child,” to name just a few.

Jernigan ended his concert with the song that inspired the title of his album, “God in the
Waiting.”

After the concert ended, many decided to hang around, talking and connecting with each other.

“I stumbled across Seth's music back in July, and he had posted videos of himself just singing
along to the Psalms.” Student Government President Stephen Largent said. “I thought that he
was really talented, and being student body president, I was like…we haven't had a concert here
in a long time, and so I reached out.”

“It was great!” Lillian Smith said. “I thought they were very talented singers and did a great job.
Beautiful music. I really liked how they used actual Psalms for the lyrics. I hope that they are
able to come back again to a full house. Someday. They deserved a bigger crowd.”

“I thought it would be a cool experience, since I've never been to a worship concert before,”
Jalyn Leicht said. “They are really talented singers, and I love how they are really supportive of
each other, like one would be smiling while the other was performing. I really enjoyed it! I hope
we can see them again, because it was really fun!”

“It was very intimate, very togetherness,” Jernigan said. “It just felt really good. It felt like
everyone was worshiping, like everyone was engaged, and when that happens, I'm more
engaged.”

So to answer the opening question: it is engagement that determines a concert, not the number of
people that attend.

“This concert was a reminder that God is faithful no matter what,” Seth Rieske said. “That no
matter what dark times you're in, no matter what you're going through, God is there. God is with
you. And even in the waiting, God is there, at your side, no matter what.”
